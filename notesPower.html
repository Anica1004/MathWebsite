Powers and Exponential Laws

2. Powers and Exponential Laws

    2.1 What are Powers?
Have you ever wondered, "How do I simplify expressions like: \(2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2\)?"
This is where powers come quite handy as \(2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 \) can be expressed in powers as \(2^4\)!

A power (or exponent) represents the number of times a base number is multiplied by itself.
It consists of two parts: the base and the exponent.
The exponent indicates how many times the base is multiplied by itself.

Example: \(3^2\)

- 3 is the Base
- 2 is the Exponent
- This expression can be read as "3 raised to the power of 2"
- It means \(3 \times 3\)

Example: How could you represent \(3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3\) as a power?
Identify the base and the exponent.

Solution:

- \(3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 = 3^5\)
- 3 is the Base
- 5 is the Exponent

    2.2 Properties of Exponents: Zero, Odd, and Even Exponents

Even Exponents:

When a base is raised to an even exponent, the result is always positive, regardless of the sign of the base.

ex) \((-2)^2\) equals 4

Odd Exponents:

When a base is raised to an odd exponent, the result retains the sign of the base. If the base is positive, the result is positive, and if the base is negative, the result is negative.

ex) \((-2)^3\) equals -8

Zero Exponents:

An exponent of zero (0) results in the value of 1, regardless of the base. Any number raised to the power of zero equals 1.

ex) \((-2)^0\) equals 1

Example: Is \((-2)^4\) and -\((2)^4 \) the same?

Solution:

No it is not the same.
- This is because \((-2)^4 = 16\) and \(-(2)^4 = -16\)

    2.3 Exponent Laws

Multiplying Powers:

When multiplying powers with the same base, add the exponents to write the product as a single power.

\( (a^m)(a^n) = a^{m+n} \)

Dividing Powers:

When dividing powers with the same base, subtract the exponents to write the quotient as a single power.

\( a^m \div a^n = a^{m-n} \)

Raising a Power to an Exponent:

When a power is raised to an exponent, multiply the exponents to write the expression with a single exponent.

\( (a^m)^n = a^{mn} \)

Raising a Product to an Exponent:

When a product is raised to an exponent, you can rewrite each factor in the product with the same exponent

\( (ab)^m = a^mb^m \)

Raising a Quotient to an Exponent:

When a quotient is raised to an exponent, you can rewrite each number in the quotient with the same exponent

\( (\frac{a}{b})^n = \frac{a^n}{b^n} \)

    2.4 Word Problems

When we're learning about exponents, we often come across word problems.
Let's solve a typical question together to make things clearer!

Example: A dish holds 100 bacteria. It is known that the bacteria double in number every hour. How many bacteria will be present after 5 hours?

Step 1) Initial amount of bacteria: It is given that the dish holds 100 bacteria
Step 2) Rate of growth: It is known that the bacteria double in number every hour. So, the growth rate (or factor) is 2.
Step 3) Number of hours: We are solving the number of bacterias after 5 hours.
Putting these information together, we can calculate the final amount of bacteria.

Final amount = Initial amount × \( (\text{Growth rate})^{\text{Number of hours}} \)
- Sub in the values that we found in steps 1), 2), 3)
Final amount = 100 × \( (2)^5 = 100 \times 32 = 3200 \)

Therefore, after 5 hours, there will be 3200 bacteria present.
